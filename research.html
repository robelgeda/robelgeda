Research

    The research teams at Rutgers have given me an opportunity to explore the universe at large and microscopic scales. I am mainly working with two teams at Rutgers with physics backgrounds but very different research applications. I have been working under professor Alyson Brooks on dark matter simulations. I am currently developing software that can acutely track dark matter halos according to their mass. The Rutgers Laser Spectroscopy Laboratory explores the quantum mechanics behind crystal structures using Raman spectroscopy. I have been working on software to improve data analysis procedures and will continue to do experiments in the near future. I have included descriptions of projects I have been involved with.

Dark Matter Simulations

The team under Professor Alyson Brooks performs analysis on data from dark matter halo simulations. The current interests of the team include the role irregular galaxies play on the formation of Milky Way mass galaxies. This can be done by closely studying the dark matter halo associated with the galaxies and how they merge to form a large structure. The simulations take into account variables such as supernovae and star formation.


Sort video on galaxy formation and evolution simulation by Princeton Astrophysics.



My task has been to develop software, mostly written in IDL, to track the dark matter halos as they progress through various time steps. So far we have been able to produce results with integrity and plan on improving them. I am currently using Mathematica code (Wolfram language) to plot and display the path taken by the galaxies. I have had the liberty of developing this procedure with a large degree of freedom under the close supervision of Dr. Brooks. Since the final results are so resent, I will be able to share them after various checks and presentations.

Raman Spectroscopy

The Rutgers Laser Spectroscopy Laboratory under Professor Girsh Blumberg uses an optical table to preform experiments on crystal samples. The current interests of the lab include superconductors and topological insulators. These materials have a broad range of applications in electronics such as superconducting materials with high Curie temperatures and quantum computing. The lab has made an extensive effort in producing reliable data that can be used to measure phonons (molecular vibrations) in the crystals. I am currently involved with improving the software used in analyzing the data produced by the experiments. The main tool I have used for this task has been Mathematica. This computation language has allowed me to customize Fourier transforms between frequency and time domains according to the type of data. This is especially helpful in producing simultaneous fits and parameters with reduced residuals. I will soon be helping with Raman experiments on a new batch of topological insulators.

RASKEL: The Rutgers Astronomical Society KELT Exoplanet Lab

I am involved with undergrad efforts to identify and confirm exoplanets using resources available at Rutgers Physics. The Kilo-degree Extremely Little Telescope (KELT) is an appropriately named tiny telescope which has been custom-designed to search for transiting exoplanets. The instrument takes images of the sky every few minutes to search for the slight decrease in light which characterizes the passage of a planet in front of its star. While KELT has generated a list of candidates, they need help to do follow-up observations on their targets. Thus we will be using the 0.5 meter telescope at the Schommer Observatory to obtain light curves for these candidates to confirm whether or not they are exoplanets.
